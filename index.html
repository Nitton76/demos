Why a POS — not just a wallet?

A wallet moves money. A register runs a business.

A plain wallet is built for one person spending their own money. A shop is many hands, money that belongs to the business, and a need to know exactly what happened. That specificity is the product — and it compounds with every feature we add.
Roles, not just keys
Staff take payments into the business register — but they can't transfer funds, withdraw, or touch the business account. Only the owner moves money. A cashier never holds the treasury or the recovery phrase.
Books that reconcile
Every sale is tagged — by staff member, by day, by rail — and exportable. A wallet shows a balance; a register shows what the business actually did.
One register, many hands
Add a cashier in seconds, remove them just as fast. No shared recovery phrase, no risk that a departing employee walks off with the keys to the money.
On the roadmap: reporting dashboards · staff management & role-based permissions · shift reconciliation · multi-location registers — each one a reason a business stays, that a bare wallet can never match.

Why this matters most in poor countries

  • The unbanked get a checkout. No bank account is the norm, not the exception — Cowbolt needs none.
  • Inflation defense built in. Vendors in Argentina, Nigeria, Lebanon, Turkey can keep takings in dollars (USDT) instead of a currency losing 30–100%/yr.
  • Remittances without the 6–10% cut. Family abroad pays a link; it lands in seconds, not days, minus almost nothing.
  • No card infrastructure required. Where Visa/Mastercard acceptance barely exists, a QR on a phone leapfrogs it entirely.
  • Censorship- and seizure-resistant. Self-custody means no government or bank can freeze a small business's cash.
  • Tiny, fast, offline-friendly. The web app is a few KB — it loads on a 2G connection and a $40 handset.
