Why "23.9% capacity factor" sounds better than it is: A 20–24% capacity factor implies the plant runs ~1 in 5 hours. Anclote's actual pattern is different — it idles at minimum load almost all the time rather than cycling off. Steam plants behave this way because cold-starts are expensive, time-consuming, and stressful on equipment. It's cheaper to throttle to 70–80 MW and stay warm than to shut down. The steam turbine spins at constant 3,600 RPM whether generating 80 MW or 500 MW. The transformer hums at 60 Hz constantly. Low-frequency content is load-independent.
How to test this properly:
(1) Tonight at 6 PM = peak load → if you hear a hum from the back yard, that's the worst case. Note exactly what you hear.
(2) Return at 3 AM on a normal running night → this is the minimum-load baseline you'd live with every night.
(3) Return during a confirmed maintenance outage → this is the true ambient floor without the plant (only 3 windows in 10 months). Contact Duke Energy Florida or check the FL PSC docket for scheduled outages.
The difference between (2) and (3) = your actual nightly noise exposure from the plant.
